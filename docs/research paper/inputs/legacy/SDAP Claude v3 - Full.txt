1. Introduction
1.1 Problem Statement
Complex systems surround usâ€”from mitochondria to microprocessors, from blockchain networks to biological ecosystems. Despite their diversity, these systems share recurring structural and behavioral principles: feedback loops, modular composition, emergent properties, hierarchical organization. Practitioners in biology, engineering, computer science, and organizational design repeatedly rediscover these principles, often using domain-specific terminology that obscures their underlying unity.
Several frameworks have attempted to codify design principles within specific domains. TRIZ (Theory of Inventive Problem Solving) offers 40 inventive principles for technical systems. The Gang of Four catalogued 23 design patterns for object-oriented software. Christopher Alexander proposed pattern languages for architecture. Systems thinking provides concepts like feedback, emergence, and homeostasis. Yet no unified framework exists that:

Spans multiple domains (biological, technical, organizational, informational, physical)
Provides a generative grammar for combining principles
Offers quantitative precision for physical systems
Enables systematic identification of unexplored design possibilities

The absence of such a framework leads to fragmented knowledge, redundant discovery, and missed opportunities for cross-domain innovation.
1.2 Research Question
Can we construct a "periodic table" of system design principlesâ€”a unified framework that classifies atomic design principles, provides operators for combining them into complex configurations, and extends to physical/mechanical domains with quantitative precision?
This question draws inspiration from chemistry's periodic table, which not only classifies elements but also predicts properties based on position and enables systematic exploration of combinations. We ask whether a similar structure can be achieved for the more abstract domain of system design.

2. Background and Related Work
2.1 TRIZ and Inventive Principles
TRIZ, developed by Genrich Altshuller from analysis of thousands of patents, provides 40 inventive principles for resolving technical contradictions. Its key insight is that inventive problems share structural patterns across domains. SDAP builds on TRIZ by: (1) adopting atomic principles that combine to solve complex problems; (2) incorporating three TRIZ-inspired operators (Resource, Ideality, Contradiction); (3) using TRIZ's 40 principles as validation benchmark. However, SDAP operates at higher abstraction, seeking principles transcending technical systems to include biological, organizational, and informational domains.
2.2 Design Patterns and Pattern Languages
The Gang of Four's design patterns (Gamma et al., 1994) codified 23 recurring solutions for object-oriented software. Christopher Alexander's pattern language (1977) proposed similar approach for architecture. SDAP differs by seeking atomic principles rather than patterns. Patterns are solutions to recurring problems; atomic principles are fundamental building blocks from which patterns emerge. In SDAP terms, a design pattern would be expressed as formula combining multiple atomic principles via operators.
2.3 Metamodeling and MOF
The Object Management Group's Meta-Object Facility (MOF) defines a four-layer metamodeling architecture: M0 (instances), M1 (models), M2 (metamodels), M3 (meta-metamodels). SDAP adopts this layered structure but with different content: our meta-meta-model contains operators rather than metaclasses, and our model layer contains design principles rather than domain models.
2.4 Systems Thinking and Cybernetics
Systems thinking (Meadows, 2008; Forrester, 1961) and cybernetics (Wiener, 1948; Ashby, 1956) provide foundational concepts: feedback, emergence, homeostasis, variety, requisite complexity. SDAP incorporates these as atomic principles and operators.
2.5 Physical System Modeling
Bond graphs, Lagrangian mechanics, and thermodynamics provide quantitative frameworks for physical systems. SDAP is not a simulator but a design vocabulary capturing structure and relationships, not dynamic equations. SDAP 3.0 bridges this gap partially through physical operators enabling quantitative description while maintaining high abstraction.
